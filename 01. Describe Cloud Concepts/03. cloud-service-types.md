# AZ-900 Study Guide: Module 3 — Cloud Service Types (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS)

This module covers the three main cloud service models. They sit on a sliding scale — from "you control almost everything" to "the provider controls almost everything." Understanding where each one sits is one of the most heavily tested concepts on the AZ-900 exam.

---

## 1. Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS)

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
IaaS is the most flexible cloud service model. The provider handles the physical hardware, network connectivity, and physical security. Everything else — operating systems, configuration, storage, databases — is up to you.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of IaaS like renting an empty warehouse. The landlord takes care of the building, the roof, and the security gate, but the inside is a blank space. You bring in your own shelving, equipment, and organization — arranged exactly how you want it.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**
- Maximum control and flexibility over your environment.
- You decide how the operating system, network, and storage are configured.
- Ideal when you need to replicate very specific setups.

**Common scenarios:**
- **Lift-and-shift migration:** Recreate your on-premises setup in the cloud, then move workloads over with minimal changes.
- **Testing and development:** Quickly spin up or tear down environments while keeping full control over configuration.

**Exam Takeaway:** With IaaS, the provider only covers hardware, connectivity, and physical security — you manage nearly everything running on top of it.

---

## 2. Platform as a Service (PaaS)

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
PaaS sits in the middle ground. The provider manages the physical infrastructure *and* the operating systems, middleware, development tools, and analytics services. You focus on building and running your application, not maintaining the platform underneath it.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of PaaS like renting a fully equipped commercial kitchen. The stoves, fridges, and utensils are already there and maintained for you — you just show up and cook. You don't have to worry about appliance repairs or health inspections; you focus purely on the menu.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**
- No need to worry about licensing or patching operating systems and databases.
- You focus on your application code, data, and access controls.
- Some networking and application security settings are shared between you and the provider, depending on configuration.

**Common scenarios:**
- **Development framework:** Build or customize cloud-based applications using built-in components, with scalability, high availability, and multitenancy already handled for you.
- **Analytics or business intelligence:** Use provided tools to mine data, find patterns, and predict outcomes without managing the underlying infrastructure.

**Exam Takeaway:** PaaS removes the burden of maintaining the platform itself, so developers can focus purely on building applications.

---

## 3. Software as a Service (SaaS)

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
SaaS is a fully developed, ready-to-use application that you rent or subscribe to. The provider manages essentially the entire stack — infrastructure, platform, and the application itself.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of SaaS like staying at a hotel. Everything is already set up — furniture, cleaning, maintenance, room service. You just check in and use the room. You're not responsible for fixing anything; you simply use what's provided.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**
- The least flexible model, but by far the easiest to get up and running.
- Requires the least technical knowledge to use effectively.
- You mainly manage your data, identity and access settings, and device access posture — the provider handles the rest.

> **Practical example:** A team using a SaaS collaboration platform can focus on onboarding users, setting access controls, and governing their data, while the provider takes care of infrastructure patching, application updates, and uptime.

**Common scenarios:**
- Email and messaging.
- Productivity applications.
- Finance and expense tracking.

**Exam Takeaway:** SaaS has the lowest operational overhead for you — the provider manages almost everything except your data and who has access to it.

---

## Quick Comparison Table

| Model | Provider Manages | You Manage | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **IaaS** | Hardware, network, physical security | OS, storage, apps, most configuration | Lift-and-shift migrations, custom test/dev environments |
| **PaaS** | Infrastructure + OS, middleware, dev tools | App code, data, access controls | App development, analytics/BI |
| **SaaS** | Nearly everything (infrastructure, platform, app) | Data, identity, device access | Email, productivity tools, finance apps |

## Quick Module Recap

- **IaaS** = renting the building blocks; maximum control, maximum responsibility.
- **PaaS** = renting a ready-to-use platform; focus on building your app, not maintaining infrastructure.
- **SaaS** = renting a finished product; lowest effort, lowest flexibility.
- Responsibility shifts progressively from you (IaaS) toward the provider (SaaS) — but you always retain control of your data, identity, and access.
