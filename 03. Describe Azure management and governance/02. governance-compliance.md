# AZ-900 Study Guide: Module 10 — Governance and Compliance in Azure

This module covers the tools Azure gives you to stay in control of your data, resources, and compliance posture — from data governance across your entire estate down to preventing accidental deletion of a single resource.

---

## 1. Microsoft Purview

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
Microsoft Purview is a family of data governance, risk, and compliance solutions that gives you a single, unified view into your data — whether it lives on-premises, across multiple clouds, or in SaaS applications.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of Purview like a librarian for your entire organization's data, no matter where it's physically stored. It knows what's on every shelf, flags which documents are sensitive, and can trace exactly where a piece of information came from and where it's gone.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**
- Provides automated data discovery, sensitive data classification, and end-to-end data lineage.
- Keeps you up to date on your entire data landscape, not just your Azure resources.

**Two main solution areas:**

### Risk and Compliance Solutions

Built around Microsoft 365 services like Teams, OneDrive, and Exchange, these solutions help your team:
- Protect sensitive data across clouds, apps, and devices.
- Identify data risks and manage regulatory compliance requirements.
- Get started with regulatory compliance efforts.

### Unified Data Governance

Manages data across Azure, SQL and Hive databases, on-premises systems, and even other clouds like Amazon S3. This helps your team:
- Build an up-to-date map of your entire data estate, including classification and lineage.
- Identify where sensitive data lives.
- Create a secure environment for data consumers to find valuable data.
- Generate insights into how data is stored and used.
- Manage access to data securely at scale.

**Exam Takeaway:** Microsoft Purview isn't limited to Azure — it's designed to give visibility across on-premises, multicloud, and SaaS data all at once.

## 2. Azure Policy

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
Azure Policy lets you create, assign, and manage rules that control or audit your resources, keeping your environment compliant with your own standards.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of Azure Policy like a building code inspector who checks every new construction project against the rules — and can even flag or fix violations in buildings that were built before the code existed.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**
- Evaluates resources and highlights any that aren't compliant.
- Can prevent noncompliant resources from being created in the first place.
- Applies to individual resources, resource groups, subscriptions, and beyond — and policies are **inherited**, so a policy set at a resource group level automatically applies to everything inside it.

**How it works:**
- Comes with built-in policy and initiative definitions for Storage, Networking, Compute, Security Center, and Monitoring.
- Policies are invoked both when a resource is created (e.g., a VM of a disallowed size) and continuously evaluated afterward, including resources that existed before the policy was created.
- Can automatically remediate noncompliant resources — for example, adding a missing required tag.
- Exceptions can still be granted, so you retain full control.
- Integrates with Azure DevOps, applying pipeline policies during pre- and post-deployment phases.

**Guardrails for AI-assisted changes:**
Even if changes come from Copilot recommendations or automated agents, Azure Policy still enforces your standards — required tags, allowed locations, approved resource SKUs, and security baselines all still apply, regardless of how the change was proposed.

### Azure Policy Initiatives

**What it is:** A way of grouping related individual policies together under one larger compliance goal.

> **Example:** The "Enable Monitoring in Azure Security Center" initiative includes policies like monitoring unencrypted SQL databases, monitoring OS vulnerabilities, and monitoring missing endpoint protection — over 100 policy definitions in total, all tracked under one initiative.

**Exam Takeaway:** A single **policy** enforces one rule; an **initiative** groups multiple related policies together to track compliance toward a broader goal.

## 3. Resource Locks

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
A resource lock prevents a resource from being accidentally deleted or changed — even by users who would otherwise have permission to do so.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of it like a child safety lock on a cabinet. Even the adult who has every right to open that cabinet still has to remove the lock first — it's an extra deliberate step that prevents accidental mishaps.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**
- Adds protection beyond Azure RBAC, since even authorized users can accidentally delete critical resources.
- Can be applied to individual resources, resource groups, or an entire subscription.
- Locks are **inherited** — a lock on a resource group applies to everything inside it.

**Two types of locks:**

| Lock Type | What It Prevents |
|---|---|
| **Delete** | Resource can still be read and modified, but not deleted |
| **ReadOnly** | Resource can be read, but not modified or deleted (similar to the Reader role) |

**Managing locks:** Through the Azure portal, PowerShell, Azure CLI, or Resource Manager templates — in the portal, locks are found in the Locks section of a resource's Settings pane.

**Making changes to a locked resource:** You must first remove the lock, then perform the action. This applies regardless of RBAC permissions — even a resource owner must remove the lock first.

**Exam Takeaway:** Resource locks apply on top of RBAC, not instead of it — being an Owner doesn't bypass a lock; you still have to remove it first.

## 4. The Service Trust Portal

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
The Microsoft Service Trust Portal provides access to content, tools, and resources detailing Microsoft's security, privacy, and compliance practices for its cloud services.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of it like a vendor's audited compliance binder — instead of taking their word for it, you can review the actual documentation showing how they protect your data and meet regulatory standards.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**
- Contains details of Microsoft's implementation of controls and processes protecting cloud services and customer data.
- Some resources require signing in with a Microsoft Entra work or school account, plus accepting a non-disclosure agreement for compliance materials.

**Accessing it:** Available at servicetrust.microsoft.com, with a main menu including:

| Menu Item | What It Does |
|---|---|
| **Service Trust Portal** | Quick link back to the home page |
| **My Library** | Save (pin) documents for quick access, with update notifications |
| **All Documents** | The central landing place for all available documents |

> **Note:** Reports and documents remain available to download for at least 12 months after publishing, or until a newer version replaces them.

**Exam Takeaway:** The Service Trust Portal is where you go to review Microsoft's own compliance documentation — not a tool for managing your own resources.

## Quick Module Recap

- **Microsoft Purview** provides unified data governance and risk/compliance visibility across on-premises, multicloud, and SaaS data.
- **Azure Policy** enforces and audits compliance rules across resources, with inheritance and the option to group related policies into **initiatives**.
- **Resource locks** (Delete or ReadOnly) prevent accidental changes or deletions, applying on top of RBAC permissions.
- The **Service Trust Portal** gives you access to Microsoft's own security, privacy, and compliance documentation.
