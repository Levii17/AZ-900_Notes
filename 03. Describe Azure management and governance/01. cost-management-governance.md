# AZ-900 Study Guide: Module 9 — Azure Cost Management and Governance

This module is all about money: what drives Azure costs, how to estimate and monitor spend, and how to keep your environment organized and cost-optimized. Expect the exam to test scenario-based questions like "which pricing option fits this workload?" — so pay close attention to the decision guides.

---

## 1. Factors That Affect Costs in Azure

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
Because Azure uses a consumption-based model, you pay only for what you use — but several factors influence exactly how much that ends up being.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of your Azure bill like a utility bill. It's not just about how much electricity you use — the rate depends on your provider, your location, the time of year, and whether you've signed up for any special plans.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**
Understanding these factors helps you predict and control spend before it surprises you on an invoice.

**Key cost factors:**

| Factor | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Resource type** | The type of resource, its settings, and the region all affect price (e.g., storage type, VM processor/cores, licensing) |
| **Consumption** | Pay-as-you-go means more usage costs more; discount options exist for predictable workloads |
| **Maintenance** | Leftover resources (like storage or networking tied to a deprovisioned VM) can keep costing money if not cleaned up |
| **Geography** | Region affects both resource cost (power, labor, taxes) and network traffic cost (moving data across continents costs more than within one) |
| **Subscription type** | Some subscriptions include free credits or usage allowances (e.g., Azure free trial) |
| **Azure Marketplace** | Third-party solutions may add vendor costs on top of standard Azure charges |

### A Closer Look: Consumption

- **Pay-as-you-go:** Pay only for what you use in a billing cycle — maximum flexibility, and cost scales directly with usage.
- **Reservations:** Commit to specific resources for one or three years for a discount (VMs, databases, storage).
- **Azure savings plan for compute:** Commit to an hourly spend amount instead of a specific VM type; Azure automatically applies the best price to eligible usage.
- **Azure Spot Virtual Machines:** Use unused Azure capacity at a reduced price, ideal for interruptible workloads — but Azure can reclaim that capacity when needed.

### A Closer Look: Network Traffic and Geography

- **Billing zones** are geographical groupings of Azure regions used specifically for data-transfer pricing — different from availability zones.
- Some inbound data transfers (into Azure) are free; outbound transfers (leaving Azure) are priced based on zone.
- Moving data within a single continent (e.g., within Europe) is generally cheaper than moving it across continents (e.g., Europe to Asia).

**Exam Takeaway:** Cost isn't just about resource type — region, network traffic direction, and subscription type all factor into your final bill.

## 2. The Pricing Calculator

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
An online tool that estimates the cost of provisioning Azure resources before you actually deploy anything.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of it like a grocery store's online cart total before checkout — you can add and remove items to see the estimated cost, without actually buying anything yet.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**
- Estimate costs for individual resources, a full solution, or an example scenario.
- Nothing is provisioned and you're never charged just by using the calculator — it's for planning purposes only.
- Lets you compare estimates across regions, service tiers, and redundancy options before committing to an architecture.

> **Practical example:** Planning a new web application, you could model an App Service plan, managed database, and storage in the calculator, compare monthly costs across regions and tiers, then use that as a baseline for budget approval.

> **Note:** The Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) calculator has been retired — the pricing calculator is now the primary estimation tool.

**Exam Takeaway:** The pricing calculator gives estimates only — it doesn't provision resources or generate charges.

## 3. Microsoft Cost Management

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
A set of tools that helps you analyze, monitor, and manage your actual Azure spend, so unexpected or accidental resource usage doesn't surprise you on an invoice.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
If the pricing calculator is planning your grocery budget in advance, Cost Management is like checking your actual bank statement afterward — tracking what you really spent, flagging anything unusual, and helping you set spending limits going forward.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**
- Quickly check resource costs, create spend alerts, and build budgets that can even automate resource management.

### Cost Analysis

A visual feature for exploring your cloud costs — aggregated by subscription, resource group, or service, and viewed over time to estimate trends against a budget.

**Common tasks:** spotting a sudden spend increase after a deployment, identifying idle resources in nonproduction subscriptions, and checking whether tagging and budgets are working as expected.

### Cost Alerts

Three types of alerts appear in Cost Management:

| Alert Type | What It Notifies You About |
|---|---|
| **Budget alerts** | Spending reaching or exceeding a threshold you define (via Azure portal or Consumption API) |
| **Credit alerts** | Azure Prepayment (for Enterprise Agreement customers) being consumed — automatic at 90% and 100% |
| **Department spending quota alerts** | Enterprise Agreement department spending reaching a fixed quota threshold (e.g., 50% or 75%) |

> **Practical example:** Setting an alert at 80% of a monthly dev/test budget lets your team investigate and right-size resources before costs go over target.

### Budgets

A budget sets a spending limit, scoped to a subscription, resource group, service type, or other criteria. When spending hits the budget alert threshold, it triggers an alert (visible in Cost Management, and optionally emailed).

**Advanced option:** Budgets can trigger automation — for example, automatically shutting down nonproduction resources once a spending threshold is reached.

**Exam Takeaway:** Cost alerts are informational; budgets can go a step further and actually trigger automated actions when thresholds are crossed.

## 4. The Purpose of Tags

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
Resource tags are metadata (name/value pairs) attached to Azure resources, helping you organize, report on, and manage them beyond just subscriptions and resource groups.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of tags like sticky notes on filing folders — even if folders (resource groups) are already organized by project, a sticky note can add extra detail like "Owner: Finance Team" or "Environment: Production," making it easy to search across many folders at once.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**

| Use Case | How Tags Help |
|---|---|
| **Resource management** | Locate and act on resources tied to specific workloads, environments, teams, or owners |
| **Cost management and optimization** | Group resources to report on costs, allocate cost centers, track budgets, forecast spend |
| **Operations management** | Group resources by criticality to inform SLAs |
| **Security** | Classify data by security level (e.g., public vs. confidential) |
| **Governance and compliance** | Identify resources aligned with regulatory requirements (e.g., ISO 27001); enforce standards like requiring an owner tag |
| **Workload optimization and automation** | Visualize resources in complex deployments and trigger automation (e.g., via Azure DevOps) |

**A practical starter tag set:** Environment, Owner, CostCenter, and Workload — covers most day-to-day filtering, ownership lookup, and automation scoping needs.

**Managing tags:**
- Add, modify, or delete tags via the Azure portal, PowerShell, CLI, Resource Manager templates, or the REST API.
- **Azure Policy** can enforce tagging rules — requiring specific tags on new resources, or reapplying tags that were removed.
- Resources don't automatically inherit tags from subscriptions or resource groups — this lets you build custom tagging schemas at each level.

**Example tag structure:**

| Name | Value |
|---|---|
| AppName | The application the resource belongs to |
| CostCenter | Internal cost center code |
| Owner | Technical or service owner |
| Environment | "Prod," "Dev," or "Test" |
| Impact | "Mission-critical," "High-impact," or "Low-impact" |

> **Good to know:** You don't need every tag on every resource — for example, you might only tag mission-critical resources with an Impact tag, treating everything else as non-critical by default.

**Exam Takeaway:** Tags provide flexible, cross-cutting organization that doesn't automatically inherit between subscription, resource group, and resource levels — each level can be tagged independently.

## 5. Cost Optimization Options in Azure

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
Once you understand and monitor your spend, the next step is matching each workload to the pricing model that fits its usage pattern.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of it like choosing a phone plan. If your usage is steady and predictable, a fixed monthly contract (Reservations) saves money. If you want flexibility across different services but still expect regular usage, a flexible spending plan (savings plan) works well. If you can tolerate the occasional dropped call for a much cheaper rate, a discount prepaid plan (Spot pricing) might be worth the tradeoff.

**Options at a glance:**

| Option | Best For | How It Works |
|---|---|---|
| **Reservations** | Stable, predictable workloads | Commit to specific resource capacity for 1 or 3 years for discounted pricing |
| **Azure savings plan for compute** | Steady usage needing flexibility across compute services | Commit to an hourly spend amount for 1 or 3 years; Azure applies the best available price |
| **Spot pricing** | Interruptible, fault-tolerant workloads | Use unused Azure capacity at reduced prices; Azure can reclaim capacity when needed |

**Decision guide:**
- Choose **Reservations** for predictable, long-running workloads with stable resource needs.
- Choose **Azure savings plan for compute** when usage is steady, but you need flexibility across different compute services.
- Choose **Spot pricing** for fault-tolerant or interruptible workloads where lowest cost matters most.

> **Practical example:** A production SQL workload running 24/7 fits Reservations. A mixed web/API workload that shifts across compute services fits a savings plan. Batch rendering or restartable test jobs are strong Spot pricing candidates.

**Ongoing review matters:** A workload that starts as interruptible might later become business-critical and need to shift away from Spot. Likewise, a workload that stabilizes over time might benefit from moving to a commitment-based model.

**Exam Takeaway:** Match the pricing model to the workload's tolerance for interruption and predictability — Reservations for stability, savings plans for flexible steady usage, Spot for interruptible workloads.

## Quick Module Recap

- Cost is shaped by **resource type, consumption, maintenance, geography, subscription type**, and **Azure Marketplace** purchases.
- The **pricing calculator** estimates costs before deployment; it never provisions resources or charges you.
- **Cost Management** monitors actual spend through cost analysis, alerts (budget, credit, department quota), and budgets — which can even trigger automation.
- **Tags** add flexible metadata for organizing, reporting, and governing resources, independent of the resource group/subscription hierarchy.
- **Cost optimization** means matching workloads to the right pricing model: Reservations (stable), savings plans (steady but flexible), or Spot pricing (interruptible).
