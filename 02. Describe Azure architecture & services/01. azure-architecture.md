# AZ-900 Study Guide: Module 4 — Azure Architecture and Core Services

This module introduces Azure itself, then breaks down its two big building blocks: the physical infrastructure (datacenters, regions, availability zones) and the management infrastructure (resources, resource groups, subscriptions, and management groups). This hierarchy shows up constantly on the exam, so get comfortable with the order things nest in.

---

## 1. What Is Microsoft Azure?

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
Azure is Microsoft's continually growing set of cloud services. It lets you build, manage, and deploy applications on a massive global network, using whatever tools and frameworks you already prefer.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of Azure like a massive global toolshed. Whether you need a hammer (a simple virtual machine) or a fully automated workshop (AI and machine learning services), it's all available to rent — you just pick the tools that fit the job.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**
- Gives you access to hundreds of services, from simple virtual machines to advanced AI and generative AI capabilities.
- Lets you modernize gradually — start by moving existing apps to virtual machines, then evolve toward managed databases, autoscaling apps, or event-driven services as your skills grow.
- Offers specialized services like Azure AI services, Azure OpenAI Service, Azure Machine Learning, and IoT services that would be difficult or expensive to build on your own.

> **Practical example:** An organization with an internal app that experiences seasonal demand spikes could host it on virtual machines or managed app services, store data in managed databases, and monitor everything from a central dashboard — scaling up during busy periods and back down when demand drops.

**Exam Takeaway:** Azure isn't just "someone else's VMs" — it's a full ecosystem that lets you start simple and modernize one workload at a time.

## 2. Azure Physical Infrastructure

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
This is the actual physical backbone of Azure — the datacenters, and how they're grouped into regions and availability zones to keep your workloads reliable no matter where you are in the world.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of it like a global chain of warehouses. Individual warehouses (datacenters) are grouped into cities (regions), and within each city, several separate, independently powered buildings (availability zones) back each other up in case one loses power.

### Datacenters

Datacenters are facilities filled with racks of servers, along with dedicated power, cooling, and networking — similar to an on-premises datacenter, just at a much larger scale. You don't interact with individual datacenters directly; instead, they're organized into regions and availability zones.

### Regions

**What it is:** A region is a geographical area containing one or more datacenters, networked together with low-latency connections.

**Why it matters:**
- You'll usually choose a region when deploying a resource.
- Some services or VM features are only available in specific regions.
- A few global services — like Microsoft Entra ID, Azure Traffic Manager, and Azure DNS — don't require choosing a region at all.

### Availability Zones

**What it is:** Physically separate datacenters within a region, each with independent power, cooling, and networking, connected via high-speed private fiber.

**The Everyday Analogy:** It's like keeping backup generators in separate buildings rather than one — if one building goes dark, the others keep running.

**Why it matters:**
- Regions that support availability zones have a minimum of three separate zones.
- You can spread VMs, storage, and databases across zones so an outage in one doesn't take everything down.
- There's a tradeoff: duplicating resources and transferring data between zones can add cost.

**Three categories of zone-aware services:**

| Category | What It Means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Zonal** | You pin the resource to a specific zone | Virtual machines, managed disks, IP addresses |
| **Zone-redundant** | The platform automatically replicates across zones | Zone-redundant storage, SQL Database |
| **Non-regional** | Always available across Azure geographies, resilient to zone and region outages | Global services |

### Region Pairs

**What it is:** Most Azure regions are paired with another region in the same geography, at least 300 miles away, to provide extra resilience against large-scale events like natural disasters or power outages.

**Why it matters:**
- If disaster strikes one region, services can fail over to its paired region.
- One region in every pair is prioritized for restoration during a widespread outage.
- Planned updates roll out to paired regions one at a time to reduce risk.
- Data typically stays within the same geography as its pair, for compliance purposes (Brazil South is the exception).

> **Good to know:** Most region pairs back each other up in both directions, but a few — like Brazil South — are paired in only one direction. Some newer regions, such as Italy North, Poland Central, and Israel Central, don't use traditional pairing at all and instead rely on availability zones and geo-redundant storage.

### Sovereign Regions

**What it is:** Special Azure instances isolated from the main Azure environment, used for compliance or legal reasons.

**Examples:**
- **US Government regions** (US DoD Central, US Gov Virginia, US Gov Arizona, and more) — isolated instances for U.S. government agencies, operated by screened U.S. personnel.
- **China regions** (China East, China North, and more) — operated through a unique partnership between Microsoft and 21Vianet, rather than directly by Microsoft.

**Exam Takeaway:** Regions group datacenters geographically; availability zones protect against localized failures within a region; region pairs protect against failures that take out an entire region.

## 3. Azure Management Infrastructure

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
This is the organizational hierarchy that lets you structure, access, and bill for everything you deploy in Azure — from individual resources all the way up to management groups covering your entire organization.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of it like a filing system: individual documents (resources) go into labeled folders (resource groups), folders go into filing cabinets (subscriptions), and filing cabinets are organized into rooms (management groups) — with company-wide policies applying to an entire room at once.

### Resources and Resource Groups

**Resources** are the basic building blocks of Azure — anything you create, provision, or deploy, such as VMs, virtual networks, databases, or AI services.

**Resource groups** are collections of resources.

**Why it matters:**
- Every resource belongs to exactly one resource group at a time (though it can be moved between groups).
- Resource groups can't be nested, and can't be renamed after creation — so pick a clear naming convention early.
- Actions applied to a resource group affect everything inside it — deleting the group deletes all its resources, and access permissions apply to the whole group.

> **Practical example:** For a temporary dev environment, grouping everything together means you can delete the entire group when you're done. For multiple projects, separate resource groups keep each team focused only on their own resources.

### Azure Subscriptions

**What it is:** A subscription is a unit of management, billing, and scale that links to an Azure account (an identity in Microsoft Entra ID or a trusted directory).

**Why it matters:**
- An account can have multiple subscriptions, though only one is required.
- Subscriptions serve two boundary purposes:

| Boundary Type | What It Controls |
|---|---|
| **Billing boundary** | Determines how an account is billed; each subscription gets its own invoices and reports |
| **Access control boundary** | Access-management policies are applied at the subscription level |

**Common reasons to create additional subscriptions:**
- **Environments:** Separate subscriptions for sandbox, development, test, and production stages.
- **Team/workload boundaries:** Give each project its own subscription for easier cost tracking.
- **Billing:** Track costs separately, such as production versus development/testing.

### Azure Management Groups

**What it is:** A layer that sits above subscriptions, letting you organize multiple subscriptions and apply governance policies across all of them at once.

**The Everyday Analogy:** It's like setting a company-wide policy that automatically applies to every department, rather than having to set the same rule individually in each department.

**Why it matters:**
- Subscriptions inherit the conditions applied to their management group, just as resources inherit settings from their resource group.
- Management groups can be nested up to six levels deep (not counting the root or subscription level).
- Every Microsoft Entra tenant has one top-level **Tenant Root Group**, which all other management groups and subscriptions fold up into.

**Examples of management group use:**
- Apply a policy — like restricting VM locations to a specific region — across an entire "Production" management group, and it automatically applies to every subscription underneath, without being overridable.
- Grant access to multiple subscriptions at once with a single Azure RBAC assignment at the management group level, rather than scripting permissions across each subscription individually.

**Key facts:**
- A single directory supports up to 10,000 management groups.
- Each management group and subscription can have only one parent.

**Exam Takeaway:** The hierarchy flows: **Resources → Resource Groups → Subscriptions → Management Groups → Tenant Root Group.** Settings and policies flow downward through inheritance.

---

## Quick Module Recap

- Azure is a broad, expanding ecosystem — not just a place to run VMs.
- Physical infrastructure: **datacenters** sit inside **regions**; **availability zones** protect against localized outages; **region pairs** protect against region-wide disasters; **sovereign regions** exist for compliance needs.
- Management infrastructure hierarchy: **resources** belong to **resource groups**, which sit inside **subscriptions**, which can be organized under **management groups**, all folding up to a single **Tenant Root Group**.
- Policies and access settings inherit downward through this hierarchy, making it a powerful tool for governance at scale.
