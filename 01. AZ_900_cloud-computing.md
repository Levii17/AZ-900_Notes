# AZ-900 Study Guide: Module 1 — Describe Cloud Computing

Welcome in! This module is all about the *big-picture* concepts of cloud computing. Nail these fundamentals and the rest of AZ-900 gets a whole lot easier. Let's break it down.

## What Is Cloud Computing?

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
Cloud computing means renting computing power — things like servers, storage, databases, and networking — from a provider over the internet, instead of buying and running your own physical hardware. It also includes newer capabilities like IoT, machine learning, and AI.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Think of it like **renting an apartment instead of building a house**. If you need more space next month, you don't build an extension — you just rent a bigger unit. When you don't need it anymore, you give it back. No construction, no long-term commitment to bricks and mortar.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**
- **Speed:** Spin up new infrastructure in minutes, not months.
- **Elasticity:** Scale up for a big sales event, then scale back down after.
- **Global reach:** Place your services closer to customers anywhere in the world, without building new datacenters.
- **Smarter spending:** Match your spend to actual demand instead of guessing years ahead.

> **Real-world example:** A retail company expecting a huge spike in traffic during a product launch can deploy extra cloud servers *just for that week*, then scale back down afterward — no need to buy physical servers that sit idle the rest of the year.

**Exam Takeaway:** Cloud computing turns IT infrastructure from a *slow, planned purchase* into an *on-demand, elastic service*.

## The Shared Responsibility Model

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
This is the "who's responsible for what" agreement between you (the customer) and your cloud provider (like Microsoft Azure). Some things are always the provider's job, some are always yours, and some depend on what type of service you're using.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
Imagine you rent a furnished apartment. The **landlord** handles the building's structure, plumbing, and security system (the physical stuff). **You** handle locking your own door, deciding who gets a key, and keeping your personal belongings safe. If you bring your own furniture and something breaks, that's on you — but if the roof leaks, that's on the landlord.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**
- You always control your **data**, your **devices**, and your **user identities** — no matter which cloud service you pick.
- The provider always owns the **physical datacenter, network, and hosts** — you never have to worry about a broken server rack.
- Responsibility for things like **operating systems, applications, and network controls** shifts depending on the service model you choose.

**The three service models, from "you do more" to "provider does more":**

| Service Model | Who Does the Heavy Lifting |
|---|---|
| **IaaS** (Infrastructure as a Service) | You manage the most — OS, apps, patching. Provider just gives you the physical building blocks. |
| **PaaS** (Platform as a Service) | Split down the middle. Provider manages the platform; you manage your apps and data. |
| **SaaS** (Software as a Service) | Provider manages almost everything. You just use the software. |

> **Quick example:** With identity, in PaaS/SaaS you manage your own users and roles, but the provider (e.g., Microsoft Entra ID) runs the actual authentication engine underneath. With IaaS, infrastructure responsibility shifts almost entirely to the provider.

**Exam Takeaway:** No matter the service model, **you always own your data, devices, and identities** — everything else slides along a scale depending on IaaS, PaaS, or SaaS.

## Cloud Deployment Models

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
This describes *where* your cloud resources actually live: privately for just your organization, publicly available to anyone, or a mix of both.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
- **Private cloud** = owning your own house. Total control, but you pay for and maintain everything yourself.
- **Public cloud** = staying in a hotel. You share the building with other guests, but you get flexibility and never worry about maintenance.
- **Hybrid cloud** = owning a house *and* occasionally booking a hotel room when family visits unexpectedly (extra capacity when you need it).

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**

| Public Cloud | Private Cloud | Hybrid Cloud |
|---|---|---|
| No upfront hardware costs | Full control over resources & security | Maximum flexibility |
| Pay only for what you use | Your data isn't mixed with other tenants | Choose where each workload lives |
| Quick to provision/deprovision | You handle all hardware maintenance | Best of both worlds |

**Bonus concepts:**
- **Multicloud:** Using *two or more* public cloud providers at once (maybe for different features, or while migrating between providers).
- **Azure Arc:** A tool that helps you manage *all* your environments — public, private, hybrid, or multicloud — from one place.
- **Azure VMware Solution:** Lets you move existing VMware workloads into Azure without rebuilding everything from scratch.

**Exam Takeaway:** Public = shared/rented, Private = owned/dedicated, Hybrid = both connected together — and multicloud means juggling more than one *public* provider.

## The Consumption-Based Model

**What it is (Simple Definition):**
This is cloud computing's "pay only for what you use" pricing approach — no big upfront purchase required.

**The Everyday Analogy:**
It's just like your **electricity bill**. You don't buy a power plant to have lights in your house — you pay only for the electricity you actually consume each month. Use more, pay more. Use less, pay less.

**Why it matters (Key Benefit):**
- **No upfront costs** for hardware or datacenter space.
- **No wasted spend** on unused capacity sitting idle.
- **Scale up** instantly when demand increases.
- **Scale down** just as easily when demand drops.

**The old way vs. the cloud way:**

| Traditional Datacenter | Cloud-Based Model |
|---|---|
| Guess future needs *in advance* | Adjust resources to *actual* demand |
| Overestimate = wasted money | Pay only for what you use |
| Underestimate = poor performance | Scale out at peak, scale in after |
| Fixing capacity takes weeks/months | Add or remove capacity in minutes |

**A quick finance term to remember:**
- **CapEx** (Capital Expenditure) = big upfront spending on physical stuff (traditional datacenters).
- **OpEx** (Operational Expenditure) = ongoing spending over time (cloud computing falls here).

**Exam Takeaway:** Cloud computing is classified as **OpEx, not CapEx** — you're renting a service over time, not buying a physical asset.

---

## Quick Module Recap

- Cloud computing = renting IT services on-demand over the internet.
- Shared Responsibility Model = you always own data/devices/identities; everything else depends on IaaS/PaaS/SaaS.
- Deployment models = Private (own it), Public (rent it), Hybrid (mix), Multicloud (multiple public providers).
- Consumption-based model = pay-as-you-go, classified as OpEx.
